# Creative Methodology Skill Specification

**Status:** DRAFT
**Date:** 2 March 2026
**Source:** Extracted from Adam's natural cognitive process across multiple conversations
**Purpose:** Formalise as reproducible AI skill(s) for Atlas and Observer ecosystem

## Overview

This document captures a creative methodology for navigating unknown problem spaces and arriving at novel solutions. The methodology was extracted from Adam's natural cognitive process — a process he uses instinctively but which follows identifiable structural operations that can be formalised, tested, and refined.

The core insight: creativity is not random inspiration. It is a structured process of oscillation, perspective generation, convergence detection, and evaluative interrogation. The process is fractal — it applies at every scale from finding protocol primitives to designing entire systems.

---

## Core Operations

### Operation 1: Scale Oscillation

Move between the boundary (big picture) and the components (small picture) repeatedly. Each cycle refines understanding at both levels.

**How it works:**
- Start with the boundary — what is the full extent of this idea/problem?
- Zoom into the components — what are the parts? What are the smallest meaningful units?
- Zoom back out — has the boundary shifted now that you've examined the parts?
- Repeat — each cycle increases resolution without losing relationship to the whole

**Analogy:** A surveyor takes the broad triangulation first, then refines within each triangle, then refines again. Each pass increases precision. The relationship between the whole network and individual measurements is maintained at every scale.

**Key principle:** The oscillation is not just switching attention. Each zoom-in informs the next zoom-out. The boundary may change as components are understood. The components may be re-evaluated as the boundary shifts. It's a dynamic refinement, not a fixed alternation.

**Structural note:** This oscillation creates a natural 90-degree semantic angular separation — you are structurally generating different perspectives by the act of changing scale. The big picture view and the component view are inherently different angles on the same problem.

---

### Operation 2: Perspective Generation (Exploratory Walk)

Generate multiple perspectives on the problem. Critically, this is not rotating through a fixed set of viewpoints. Each perspective seeds the discovery of the next.

**How it works:**
- Look at the problem from an initial angle
- That angle reveals a new angle you hadn't considered — follow it
- The new angle reveals another — follow that too
- Keep generating perspectives until convergence begins (see Operation 3)
- The walk through perspective space is exploratory, not predetermined

**Key principle:** You don't know in advance which perspectives you need. The process of looking from angle A reveals that angle B exists. B reveals C. This is a walk through semantic space where each step informs the direction of the next step.

**What counts as a perspective:**
- A different domain (how would this look in surveying? in biology? in urban planning?)
- A different stakeholder (the user, the builder, the adversary, the community)
- A different time horizon (now, in 2 years, in 10 years, in retrospect)
- A different scale (individual, team, organisation, society)
- A self-similar reference point (someone thinking about similar problems from a different angle — e.g., comparing your approach to Daniel Miessler's to find productive deltas)

**Using self-similar reference points:** When you find someone working in a similar problem space with a slightly different angle, the delta between your perspectives becomes a rich source of new insight. The angular difference between two similar-but-not-identical viewpoints is where the productive signal lives. This is triangulation using independent observers.

---

### Operation 3: Convergence Detection (Triangulation)

Sense when perspectives are clustering toward a shape versus still scattered. This is the surveyor's least squares adjustment applied to semantic space.

**How it works:**
- Multiple perspectives are like multiple bearings on a point
- When bearings are scattered, the position isn't resolved — keep generating perspectives
- When bearings start clustering, you're approaching the shape of the answer
- The tighter the convergence, the higher the confidence
- Perspectives that don't fit are either noise OR they're telling you the model is wrong

**Key principle:** You're taking overdetermined measurements — more perspectives than you strictly need — and finding the position in idea-space that has the least total residual against all of them. The "answer" is the point that best satisfies all observations simultaneously.

**Signals of convergence:**
- Multiple independent perspectives start pointing to the same structural pattern
- New perspectives stop revealing fundamentally new information
- The idea starts feeling simple and obvious despite being non-obvious before
- You can see how the idea would expand (fractal potential)
- You can start articulating it to others and it holds up

**Signals you haven't converged:**
- Each new perspective still fundamentally changes the picture
- The idea feels complicated and forced
- You can't explain it simply
- It only works from one angle

### Operation 4: Evaluative Interrogation

**This is the critical nuance that separates mechanical synthesis from genuine creativity.**

Once convergence produces a candidate position (a potential answer, motif, or set of primitives), there is an evaluative layer that holds the candidate up against the bigger picture intent and asks qualitative questions.

**This is NOT just least squares convergence.** The triangulation finds a candidate. The evaluative interrogation determines whether that candidate has genuine generative potential or is merely a tidy compromise.

**How it works:**
- Take the converged candidate
- Hold it against the boundary (the bigger picture intent from Operation 1)
- Ask evaluative questions (see below)
- Based on answers, either accept, modify, or reject the candidate
- If rejected or modified, feed the new understanding back into Operations 1-3 and iterate

**Core evaluative questions (known so far — this list is incomplete and should grow through use):**

*Generative potential:*
- Does this candidate have fractal potential? Will it expand into useful structure at larger scales?
- Or is it a dead end — tidy but sterile?
- If I place this motif into a completely different domain, does it generate meaningful new structure?

*Primitive assessment:*
- Are the existing primitives from both perspective sets sufficient?
- Is something missing that neither set provided?
- Do any primitives need transformation — not just selection or combination, but modification into a slightly different form?
- Is this just a better mixing of existing primitives, or does it require genuinely new elements?

*Integrity checks:*
- Does this serve the larger intent, or has the process drifted?
- Does this respect the values and constraints of the project? (sovereignty, simplicity, community benefit)
- Would this survive contact with messy reality, or is it only elegant in theory?
- Is this just cleverness, or does it have weight?

*Predictive power:*
- Does this candidate predict things we haven't seen yet? (Like Mendeleev's gaps)
- Does it suggest a missing element that should exist but doesn't?
- Does it explain something we previously couldn't explain?

*Simplicity test:*
- When I look at this, does it feel simple despite being the product of complex thinking?
- Could I explain the core of it in a few sentences?
- Does it have that quality where it seems obvious in retrospect?

**Key principle:** These questions are not a checklist to be mechanically executed. They are probes that the process surfaces at the right moment. Different problems will require different questions. The skill should surface relevant questions contextually, not dump all of them every time.

**Important nuance on primitive merging:** The evaluative step is where creativity actually happens. It's not taking primitives from perspective A and primitives from perspective B and averaging them. It's:
1. Analysing both sets to understand what each truly offers
2. Identifying the angular difference — what each set has that the other doesn't
3. Asking: what is genuinely beneficial from both?
4. Sometimes selecting the best from each (recombination)
5. Sometimes modifying primitives to better serve the intent (transformation)
6. Sometimes realising entirely new primitives are needed (creation)
7. Testing the result against the bigger picture boundary

## The Meta-Pattern

This methodology is itself fractal. The process of extracting it followed the same operations:

- **Scale oscillation:** Moving between "I do two things" (big picture) and specific examples of each (components), refining with each pass
- **Perspective generation:** Looking at the process from the lens of surveying, Git, Daniel's framework, physics theories, fractals — each angle revealing new structure
- **Convergence:** The four operations crystallising out of multiple conversational passes
- **Evaluative interrogation:** "Is this complete? No — there's a nuance in the evaluation step that isn't captured yet. The process isn't just mechanical convergence."

This self-similarity is a signal that the methodology captures something real rather than being an imposed framework.

## Implementation Notes for Atlas

### Skill Structure Options

**Option A: Single skill with phases**
One skill that walks through all four operations sequentially with iteration loops. Simpler to invoke but may be heavy for small problems.

**Option B: Two complementary skills**
- Skill 1: "Oscillate and Generate" — handles scale oscillation and perspective generation (the divergent operations)
- Skill 2: "Converge and Evaluate" — handles convergence detection and evaluative interrogation (the convergent operations)
These could be invoked together or independently. More modular.

**Option C: Cognitive behaviour (default mode)**
Rather than explicit skills invoked on demand, encode the methodology as Atlas's default approach to any novel problem. Atlas always oscillates, always generates perspectives, always checks for convergence, always evaluates candidates before committing.

**Recommendation:** Start with Option B for testing. If it proves effective, evolve toward Option C as a default cognitive behaviour.

### Testing Approach

1. Take a real unsolved problem
2. Give it to Atlas WITH the skill loaded
3. Give the same problem to Atlas WITHOUT the skill
4. Compare outputs qualitatively: does the skill version produce something simpler, more generative, more surprising?
5. Observe which evaluative questions Atlas surfaces — do they match what Adam would ask?
6. Refine the skill based on observations
7. Repeat with different problem types

### Refinement Process

The evaluative question set is explicitly incomplete. It should grow through use:
- Each time the methodology is applied to a real problem, observe which questions Adam asks that aren't in the current set
- Add them to the skill
- Over time, the skill becomes a more complete encoding of the creative methodology
- The skill refines itself through the process it describes
