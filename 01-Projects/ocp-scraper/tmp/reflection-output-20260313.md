## What the four pillars memo underweights

### 1. External legibility is uneven across pillars

- Governance is publicly legible because constitutions, policies, and approval flows are easy to publish.
- State-machine and buffer doctrines are often embedded in broader runtime systems rather than named directly.
- The memo currently treats the four pillars as more symmetric evidence classes than they are.

### 2. Governance needs both executable rules and human-legible authority

- `OPA`-style policy engines and constitution-style artifacts suggest that slow-path governance needs both machine enforcement and social legitimacy.
- The current memo captures gating well, but underemphasizes the dual artifact requirement.

### 3. Search infrastructure shapes what counts as evidence

- OCP is good at harvesting structural neighbors.
- OCP is currently weak as an exact phrase-verification engine for newly coined or locally specialized motif language.
- For motif work, absence of phrase match should not be confused with absence of structural evidence.

## Process lessons about OCP as motif evidence infrastructure

- Treat OCP scrape output as triangulation material, not as direct confirmation of memo terminology.
- Expect semantic center drift when the query names a precise mechanism but the ecosystem lacks stable shared labels.
- Exact phrase search and corpus growth are currently decoupled: the corpus can get richer while phrase-level confirmation still fails.
- arXiv silence is itself signal: these pillars appear more grounded in engineering practice and governance craft than in public academic naming.

## Supported refinement candidate

### Semantic center drift

- When a query names a precise structural mechanism without a broadly shared external label, retrieval expands outward into adjacent and more publicly legible artifacts.
- In this session, drift moved from runtime mechanisms toward catalogs/frameworks, and from approval gates toward broader governance texts.
- This is not yet strong enough for a promoted new motif, but it is a credible refinement candidate for future validation.

## What we learned that should feed forward

- Future pillar evidence collection should search for operational exemplars, not just memo-congruent wording.
- Governance evidence should include both constitutions and enforcement proxies.
- Buffer evidence likely needs more concrete systems queries than this scrape used.
- Retrieval itself may need a dual-speed pattern: exploratory semantic expansion first, stricter structural filtering second.

## Final reflection

The four pillars remain structurally plausible. The scrape session did not disconfirm them. It revealed instead that:

- our design language is sharper than the ecosystem's shared naming,
- governance is easier to evidence than runtime internals,
