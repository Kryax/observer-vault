## The d3 test

A candidate instance is genuinely d3 (not d2) if and only if:

1. It operates on **self-referential generators themselves**, not on self-referential processes directly
2. Its output is a **new kind of self-modifying mechanism**, not a modification produced by an existing mechanism
3. Removing it eliminates the system's capacity to **produce new ways of self-modifying**, not just its capacity to self-modify

The critical distinction: d2 is a feedback loop that modifies itself. d3 is what produces new kinds of feedback loops. d2 steers; d3 builds new steering mechanisms.

## Candidate: Recursive Generativity

**Working name**: Recursive Generativity

**Domain-independent description**: A process that generates new kinds of self-referential generators. The system does not merely self-modify (d1), nor merely operate a mechanism that produces self-modifications (d2). It produces new mechanisms for self-modification — it modifies its own capacity for self-modification. The "jerk" of recursion: the rate of change of the rate of change of self-referential change.

### Instance 1: Mathematical Logic — Ordinal Analysis and Reflection Principles

- **Domain**: Mathematical Logic / Proof Theory
- **Process**: Godel's incompleteness theorems show that any sufficiently strong formal system cannot prove its own consistency. A *reflection principle* is a statement asserting the soundness of a formal system — it is a self-referential generator (d2) that extends the system's provable truths. But the *transfinite iteration of reflection principles along ordinal notations* is a d3 operation. At each ordinal stage, a new reflection principle is generated that was not available at any prior stage. The process does not merely apply reflection (d2) but generates new reflection principles indexed by ordinals — it is a process that produces new kinds of self-referential validators. Turing's (1939) ordinal logics, Feferman's (1962) transfinite progressions of theories, and the proof-theoretic ordinal analysis program all instantiate this: a hierarchy where each level generates the self-referential capacity that the previous level lacked.
- **Derivative order justification**: d0 = a formal system refers to itself (Godel numbering). d1 = the system's theorems change through self-referential reasoning. d2 = a reflection principle systematically generates new truths via self-referential soundness assertions. **d3 = the ordinal iteration generates new reflection principles — it is a process that produces new self-referential generators, not merely a self-referential generator itself.** Each ordinal step creates a genuinely new kind of self-validation that was impossible at all prior steps. The hierarchy-generating process is the d3 element.
- **Source**: `ocp:sep/self-reference` (trust: 0.617), `ocp:sep/recursive-functions` (trust: 0.643), `ocp:sep/logic-higher-order` (trust: 0.619)
- **Key references**: Turing (1939) "Systems of Logic Based on Ordinals"; Feferman (1962) "Transfinite Recursive Progressions of Axiomatic Theories"; Godel (1931); Franzen (2004) *Inexhaustibility*; SEP entries "Self-Reference and Paradox", "Recursive Functions", "Second-order and Higher-order Logic"

### Instance 2: Epistemology — Wide Reflective Equilibrium as Self-Amending Method

- **Domain**: Epistemology / Philosophy of Science
- **Process**: Narrow reflective equilibrium (Rawls 1971) adjusts principles to match judgments — this is d1 self-referential change (the moral framework modifies itself by consulting its own outputs). Wide reflective equilibrium extends this by considering "all possible descriptions" and background theories — this is d2 (a generator of self-referential modifications, because the method itself is now an engine that systematically produces principle-judgment adjustments). But the truly radical move, articulated by DePaul (1993) and implicit in the "wide" reading of Rawls, is that reflective equilibrium can revise *the method of reflective equilibrium itself*. The deliberator can undergo "moral conversion" — a comprehensive change of mind that transforms not just principles and judgments but the very criteria for what counts as equilibrium. When the method revises its own criteria for revision, it is d3: it generates a new self-modifying mechanism, not merely a new modification.
- **Derivative order justification**: d0 = a moral framework exists and refers to itself. d1 = judgments and principles mutually adjust. d2 = the method of reflective equilibrium systematically generates adjustments. **d3 = the method revises its own revision-criteria, producing a new kind of reflective equilibrium.** DePaul's "radical reflective equilibrium" explicitly allows the method to replace itself. This is not the method operating (d2) but the method being replaced by a new method (d3).
- **d3 vs d2 risk assessment**: MODERATE. One could argue that "the method revising its own criteria" is just the method operating at a higher level — still d2 with more scope. The d3 claim depends on whether the revision produces a genuinely new *kind* of self-referential generator or merely extends the existing one. The DePaul reading (moral conversion as discontinuous break) supports d3; the Rawlsian reading (provisional fixed points within a single method) may collapse to d2.
- **Source**: `ocp:sep/reflective-equilibrium` (trust: 0.481)
- **Key references**: Rawls (1971) *A Theory of Justice* pp. 19-21, 48-49; DePaul (1993) *Balance and Refinement*; Elgin (1996) *Considered Judgment*; Daniels (1996) *Justice and Justification*; Goodman (1965) *Fact, Fiction, and Forecast*; SEP "Reflective Equilibrium"

### Instance 3: Machine Learning — Meta-Meta-Learning (Learning to Learn to Learn)

- **Domain**: Machine Learning / AI
- **Process**: Standard learning modifies model parameters given data (d0/d1 self-referential change — the model updates itself). Meta-learning ("learning to learn") produces learning algorithms — it is a d2 self-referential generator (MAML, Reptile, etc. generate new learning procedures). **Meta-meta-learning** goes further: it searches over the space of meta-learning algorithms themselves, producing new kinds of learning-to-learn procedures. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) applied to the meta-learner, or evolutionary strategies that evolve the meta-learning objective function, operate at d3. The system does not merely learn (d1) or learn how to learn (d2) — it modifies the mechanism by which it learns how to learn. Neuroevolution (Real et al. 2019, "Regularized Evolution for Image Classifier Architecture Search") exemplifies this when the evolutionary process itself is subject to meta-optimization: the mutation operators, selection pressures, and fitness landscapes are themselves evolved.
- **Derivative order justification**: d0 = a model has parameters (self-referential structure). d1 = gradient descent modifies parameters. d2 = meta-learning produces new learning algorithms. **d3 = meta-meta-learning produces new meta-learning algorithms — the system generates new kinds of self-modifying generators.** The NAS-over-meta-learner pattern, or evolving the evolutionary search process itself, is the d3 element.
- **d3 vs d2 risk assessment**: MODERATE-HIGH. In practice, most "meta-meta-learning" systems have a fixed outer loop that optimizes over meta-learners — the outer loop itself is not self-modifying. A truly d3 system would need the outer loop to modify itself. Pure NAS over meta-learners is arguably just d2 with an additional layer of nesting, not genuinely d3 unless the search process itself evolves. The strongest d3 examples are self-referential evolutionary systems where the mutation operators are themselves subject to mutation (Schmidhuber's Godel Machine concept).
- **Source**: `ocp:github/continualai--continual-learning-papers` (trust: 0.421), `ocp:github/alro10--awesome-deep-neuroevolution` (trust: 0.311)
- **Key references**: Schmidhuber (2003) "Godel Machines: Self-Referential Universal Problem Solvers"; Real et al. (2019) "Regularized Evolution for Image Classifier Architecture Search"; Hospedales et al. (2022) "Meta-Learning in Neural Networks: A Survey"; Finn et al. (2017) MAML (as d2 baseline); Clune (2019) "AI-Generating Algorithm"

### Instance 4: Biology — Evolutionary Transitions in Individuality (ETI)

- **Domain**: Evolutionary Biology / Major Transitions
- **Process**: A self-replicating organism modifies itself through mutation and selection (d1). The genetic regulatory architecture (gene regulatory networks, epigenetic mechanisms) generates self-modifications systematically (d2 — the organism has a mechanism for producing heritable variation). A *major evolutionary transition in individuality* (Maynard Smith & Szathmary 1995) is a d3 event: it generates a new *kind* of self-modifying entity. When single-celled organisms transitioned to multicellularity, the result was not a modification within the existing self-referential system (d1) or a new modification-generator within it (d2). It was the creation of an entirely new level of self-referential organization — a new entity (the multicellular organism) with its own novel mechanisms of self-modification (cell differentiation, developmental programming, immune self/non-self discrimination). The transition generates new generators: multicellularity creates developmental biology, which did not exist as a self-modifying mechanism prior to the transition.
- **Derivative order justification**: d0 = a self-replicating entity exists. d1 = it changes via mutation/selection. d2 = gene regulatory networks systematically generate heritable variation. **d3 = a major transition creates a new kind of entity with new kinds of self-modification mechanisms.** The transition from single-cell to multicellular life did not modify existing self-referential mechanisms — it generated fundamentally new ones (developmental gene regulatory networks, apoptosis as self-referential dissolution, immune self-recognition). Each major transition (RNA world to DNA/protein, prokaryote to eukaryote, single-cell to multicellular, individual to eusocial) is a d3 event.
- **d3 vs d2 risk assessment**: LOW. This is the strongest d3 instance. The major transitions clearly produce new *kinds* of self-referential generators rather than new modifications within existing generators. The self-referential mechanisms that exist after the transition (developmental programming, immune recognition) are categorically different from those that existed before. This is not "more of the same self-modification" — it is the creation of new self-modification machinery.
- **Source**: Domain knowledge (no strong OCP representation — GitHub/SEP scrapes did not surface major transitions directly)
- **Key references**: Maynard Smith & Szathmary (1995) *The Major Transitions in Evolution*; Michod (1999) *Darwinian Dynamics*; Szathmary (2015) "Toward major evolutionary transitions theory 2.0"; Godfrey-Smith (2009) *Darwinian Populations and Natural Selection*; Calcott & Sterelny (eds. 2011) *The Major Transitions in Evolution Revisited*

### Instance 5: Constitutional Law — Self-Amending Amendment Procedures

- **Domain**: Constitutional Law / Political Theory
- **Process**: A constitution is a self-referential document — it governs the system that produces it (d0). Constitutional amendments modify the constitution according to its own amendment procedure (d1 — self-referential change). The amendment procedure itself is a self-referential generator: it systematically produces constitutional modifications (d2). But when a constitution *amends its own amendment procedure* — as has occurred in multiple constitutional histories — this is a d3 operation. The system generates a new mechanism for generating self-modifications. Article V of the U.S. Constitution could in principle be used to amend Article V itself. France's Fifth Republic replaced the Fourth Republic's entire constitutional framework, including its amendment procedure, via the amendment procedure. The "constituent power" doctrine (Sieyes, Schmitt) recognizes a meta-constitutional authority that can replace the constitution-generating mechanism itself.
- **Derivative order justification**: d0 = a constitution exists and refers to itself. d1 = amendments change the constitution. d2 = the amendment procedure generates changes. **d3 = amending the amendment procedure generates a new kind of self-modification mechanism.** When Article V is used to change Article V, the output is not a constitutional modification (d1) or a standard product of the amendment procedure (d2) — it is a new amendment procedure, a new self-referential generator.
- **d3 vs d2 risk assessment**: LOW-MODERATE. The formal structure is clearly d3 (amending the amendment rule). The practical question is whether this has ever produced a genuinely *new kind* of self-referential generator or merely adjusted parameters of the existing one (e.g., changing ratification thresholds). The French Fourth-to-Fifth Republic transition is the strongest case: the entire constitutional framework was replaced, including the mechanism of replacement. The U.S. Article V case is weaker because Article V has never been used to amend itself (it is a theoretical possibility, not an actualized instance).
- **Source**: Domain knowledge (no direct OCP representation). Related conceptually to `ocp:sep/reflective-equilibrium` (self-amending normative frameworks).
- **Key references**: Ackerman (1991) *We the People: Foundations*; Elster (2000) *Ulysses Unbound*; Sieyes (1789) *What Is the Third Estate?*; Schmitt (1928) *Constitutional Theory*; Suber (1990) *The Paradox of Self-Amendment*; Hart (1961) *The Concept of Law* (rule of recognition)
